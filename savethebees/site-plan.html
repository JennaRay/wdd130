Home page

Bee populations have been notibly in decline since the 1990s. Some bee species have already gone extinct, while others are currently endangered (Silva). According to greenpeace.org, it is usually typical for a bee colony to lose about 5-10% of their population over the winter, 15-20% on a particullarly bad winter. In the US, that rate for bee losses has now reached around 40%, twice as much as it would usually be in a very rough winter. There are even reports of some beekeepers who have a 90% loss rate.3 There is a lot of speculation for what is causing the decline in bee populations. Some are natural causes, like the vicious varraou mites, one of the the worst pests for bees (McChesney)5 . Other factors are caused by man, such as habitat loss, use of chemical pesticides and herbicides, and climate change

Why It Matters

Where is Bee Decline a Problem? Bee populations have been reportedly in decline in many Major countries, such as China, Brazil, North America, and many countires in Europe.4 Who is Affected? The decline in the bee population affects many different groups of people. It negatively impacts agricultural farmers, as bees are necessary for the polination of most fruits, and for the seed production of some vegetables and important hays. Without bees, these plants would not be able to grow, either because they need to be pollinated to grow, or they need to be pollinated to produce seeds (Brunet, Scienta.global). The loss of alfalfa hay would impact the livestock industry greatly, as it is a common staple in feed for livestock such as cows and chickens (Brunet).1 Lastly, the loss of bees would affect the American population as a whole, as it would greatly change the american diet. Common fruits like apples, blueberries, strawberries, and avacados all rely on bee pollination. Vegetables like pumpkins, tomatoes, and cucumbers would also be effected. (DiValentino, Insider)2 According to Greenpeace.org, “Seventy out of the top 100 human food crops — which supply about 90 percent of the world’s nutrition — are pollinated by bees.” 3
